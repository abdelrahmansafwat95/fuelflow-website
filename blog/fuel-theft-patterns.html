7 fuel-theft patterns we see in Egyptian fleets — and how to stop each one.

Before we built FuelFlow, we spent six months sitting with finance and operations teams at Egyptian corporate fleets — bus operators in Greater Cairo, distribution fleets in Alexandria, agricultural fleets across the Delta. The same theft patterns came up again and again. Here are the seven that hurt the most, and what actually shuts each one down.

By Mostafa Hesham, Co-founder

1. Slip-padding at the pump

The driver fills 40 litres, then asks the station attendant to write 50 on the slip. The fleet pays the extra 10 litres in cash; the attendant keeps a cut; the driver pockets the rest. This is the single most common pattern, and it's invisible to anyone reading paper slips a week later.

How FuelFlow stops it: the pump's electronic volume reading is captured at authorization time, not at slip time. The dispenser sends us the litres dispensed directly. The driver never gets to write a number, and the attendant can't either.

2. The off-route fill

A delivery van that's supposed to be in 6th of October pulls up at a station in Maadi at 3 pm. The driver has a side hustle — maybe a personal car, maybe a friend's. The fleet pays for the fuel either way.

Egyptian fleet managers usually catch this through GPS — except the off-route fills happen at lunch breaks, when the van is technically "stopped" and nothing looks alarming on the map.

How we stop it: fuel transactions and GPS positions are stitched at the moment of authorization. If the vehicle's GPS doesn't put it within 50 metres of the authorising station, the pump won't open. No after-the-fact catch — the transaction just doesn't happen.

3. Multi-pump runs

The driver authorizes a fill, the pump opens, but only 15 litres go into the company van. The remaining capacity gets diverted to a personal car parked at the next pump over, paid from the same authorization. Two cars, one slip.

How we stop it: each authorization is bound to a single dispenser ID for a single time window. The pump closes on the company van, the company van leaves, the authorization is settled. No "open tab" the attendant can stretch.

4. Credential sharing

Driver A is on holiday. His QR code or NFC card ends up in Driver B's hands "for emergencies". Three weeks later, Driver A is back, but the QR has been used twice a day in a city he wasn't in.

How we stop it: driver identity is tied to a device-bound app session and (optionally) a selfie at the moment of fill. NFC cards are physical objects; phones with biometric login are not. We also flag impossible-travel patterns — a fill in Tanta at 9 am and one in Aswan at 11 am is mechanically impossible.

5. The "rounded" slip pile

An honest driver fills 37.4 litres. The attendant rounds it to 38. Multiply by 20 fills a day across 200 vehicles for a month and you're paying for hundreds of litres you never received.

How we stop it: we settle in three decimal places, the way the dispenser reports. Fractions add up to real money.

6. Mileage backfilling

This one isn't fuel theft per se — it's how drivers justify the fuel theft. The odometer goes up faster than the route plan says it should, so 40 extra km a day "explain" the missing 4 litres.

How we stop it: our consumption analytics flag drivers whose litres-per-100km doesn't match the fleet baseline for their vehicle type and route. Anomaly scoring runs daily and surfaces the top 5 outliers per cost centre.

7. The "internal fuel station"

Larger fleets sometimes operate their own private fuel depots. These are extremely useful — and notoriously hard to control. Without a tight authorization layer, the depot becomes a free-flow tap.

How we stop it: we treat private depots as a FuelFlow-managed station. The attendant uses the same Attendant App. Every fill is logged to a driver, a vehicle, a time, a litre count. The depot becomes auditable.

What to do today, regardless of platform

You don't need FuelFlow to start clawing back. Three things will get you most of the way:

  • Stop accepting handwritten slips. Insist on the dispenser's electronic receipt for every transaction.
  • Get GPS for every vehicle and time-stitch the GPS data to fuel timestamps. You will catch patterns 2 and 6 with this alone.
  • Publish per-vehicle consumption baselines monthly. Drivers behave differently when they know their litres-per-100km is on a chart someone reads.
